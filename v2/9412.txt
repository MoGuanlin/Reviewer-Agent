Dual-Expert Collaboration for Generalized Long-Tailed Learning

Anonymous Authors1

Abstract

The seal misclassified as a dog, due to

The Generalized Long-Tailed (GLT) distribution
features samples following a long-tailed distribution with extremely large intra-class attribute differences. Such data not only induces inter-class
learning biases in models but also causes interclass feature confusion and intra-class feature dispersion due to excessive intra-class variations. To
address this challenge, we propose a Dual-Expert
Collaboration strategy for GLT data, comprising a
Long-Tailed Learning Expert (LTLE) and a Hard
Sample Learning Expert (HSLE). Based on the
Equiangular Tight Frame (ETF) classifier, LTLE
delineates ideal feature regions to reduce interclass feature confusion, learn general features
in GLT data, and screen hard-to-learn samples.
HSLE constrains hard samples’ features within
the classifier’s neighborhood via the Boundary
ETF classifier to mitigate intra-class feature dispersion. Our method effectively addresses interclass feature confusion and intra-class feature dispersion in GLT data, significantly improves model
performance, validated by extensive experiments
with multiple metrics on the ImageNet-GLT and
MSCOCO-GLT benchmarks.

Data diversity is generally recognized as a key factor in enhancing the generalization ability and representation stability of DNNs (Trabucco et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). However, in the context of long-tailed classification, data diversity poses additional challenges—this gives rise to the generalized long-tailed distribution, which incorporates intraclass data diversity. Specifically, generalized long-tailed
data not only suffers from inter-class imbalance but also
exhibits significant intra-class attribute differences. Such
intra-class attribute imbalance further exacerbates the learning difficulty of models. For instance, a dog lying on the
grass (a typical sample with canonical attributes) is easily classified correctly, while a dog swimming in water (a
non-canonical sample with deviated attributes) poses great
challenges. During supervised learning, the model is forced
to fit the “swimming dog” to the “dog” label. This not
only distorts the already learned core features of “dog” but
also causes the representations of similar samples from tail
categories (e.g., seals) to shift towards the feature space of
“dog”. Furthermore, due to insufficient learning of certain
categories, confusing samples are more prone to misclassification—for instance, seals that resemble dogs. Figure 1
illustrates these examples, and such extensive attribute differences pose additional difficulties for long-tailed learning.

1. Introduction
Deep neural networks (DNNs) have achieved remarkable
success in image classification tasks, which heavily relies
on balanced and diverse training data to learn discriminative
core representations for each category. However, real-world
data inherently exhibits a long-tailed distribution, where
a small number of “head” categories dominate the data
volume while most “tail” categories have extremely few
samples. Such inter-class imbalance often leads to biased
optimization of DNNs, making the models overly biased
towards head categories and thus neglecting the learning of
tail categories. (Lin et al., 2023; He, 2024).

Based on the above analysis, we divide the influencing
factors of image representation into two parts: the discriminative cues corresponding to label Y should be abstracted as
class-specific components zc , which are uniquely linked to
the class label and serve as key factors for deciding whether
an image X belongs to class Y . In contrast, class-irrelevant
attribute information (e.g., object background, pose, texture, etc.) is abstracted as attribute-related variables za .

learning samples with easily distinguishable features in generalized long-tailed data. We argue that zc of all classes

We observe that the existence of attribute-related variables
za induces two types of conflicts, thereby exacerbating the
difficulty of long-tailed learning: (1) Intra-class feature
dispersion: za hinders samples from consistently learning
the underlying class-specific components zc . For instance,
if most dog samples in the training set are in grassy scenes,
the model may form a spurious association between ”grass”
and the dog label, causing dogs in non-grass scenes to fail to
acquire effective feature representations even after multiple
optimization steps; (2) Inter-class feature confusion: Such
spurious associations formed by the model, in extreme cases,
may drive the feature representation z related to ”grass” to
be overly close to the dog classifier, thereby distorting the
decision boundary and inflicting disproportionate harm on
tail classes with similar attributes or scenes.

Subsequent studies have extensively explored the neural
collapse (NC) phenomenon and its applications. For example, researchers have analyzed the formation mechanism
of this state and its impact on model generalization. Furthermore, research on neural collapse in long-tailed data
has revealed several properties worth further investigation.
Later, under imbalanced training conditions, classes with
fewer samples are less likely to undergo neural collapse, a
phenomenon referred to as ”Minority Collapse” (Fang et al.,
2021). Subsequently, the issue is addressed by preconditioning the classifier to an ETF structure, demonstrating that
under ideal conditions, neural collapse can also occur in a
few classes (Yang et al., 2022). Building on this, the classifier is directly associated with the ETF structure, aiming to
optimize the task under different conditions toward the ideal
ETF state, either through preset conditions or by inducing a
loss function (Gao et al., 2024).

4. Method
To enable the model to accurately learn the underlying classspecific components zc , we adopt a dual-expert model for
collaborative learning. Specifically, the Long-Tailed Learning Expert (LTLE) employs an Equiangular Tight Frame
(ETF) classifier to reduce inter-class feature confusion, allowing relatively easy-to-classify samples in generalized
long-tailed data to be closer to zc . Due to extensive attribute
differences, samples with significant perturbations introduced by attribute-related variables za are categorized as
hard samples.

3. Preliminaries
In the Generalized Long-Tailed Classification task, a neural
network is required to learn an effective classifier from a
training dataset Dtrain whose class frequencies follow a longtailed distribution. For simplicity, we denote the feature
extractor as hθ and write its output as hθ (x) = zx , where
zx is the feature representation of input x. The classifier
is denoted as W φ . The training objective is to learn ideal
parameters θ and φ such that, for any sample (X, Y ) from
the test set Dtest , the composed network W φ ◦ hθ maximizes the conditional likelihood p(Y | X). However, due
to the intrinsic nature of long-tailed distributions, this optimization typically induces a systematic bias in predicted
probabilities: the model tends to overestimate head classes
while underestimating tail classes.

These hard samples are subsequently handled by the Hard
Sample Learning Expert (HSLE) for secondary learning.
Based on the Boundary Equiangular Tight Frame (Boundary
ETF) classifier, HSLE adopts our proposed BCE loss to
encourage the feature representations z of hard samples
to lie within a conical region centered around zc , which
alleviates the representation instability caused by za and
addresses the problem of intra-class feature dispersion.

More importantly, Generalized Long-Tailed Classification
additionally involves intra-class attribute distribution shifts.
For example, in toilet images, white ones are far more common than gray ones; similarly, dogs appearing on grass
are much more prevalent than dogs in a pool. Ideally, the
discriminative cues for a label Y should be abstracted as
class-specific components zc , which are uniquely associated
with the class label and constitute the decisive factors determining whether an image X belongs to class Y . In contrast,
the remaining class-irrelevant attribute information should
be abstracted as attribute-related variables za . We further
assume that zc and za are tensors with the same dimensionality as z, and that the feature representation z of any image

5.1. Experimental Setting
Evaluation Protocols.To evaluate the robustness of models against class-wise imbalance, attribute-wise imbalance,
and their joint effect in Generalized Long-Tailed Classification (GLT), three protocols are designed based on two
benchmark datasets (ImageNet-GLT and MSCOCO-GLT):
1) Class-wise Long Tail (CLT) Protocol uses a class-wise
and attribute-wise long-tailed training set (Train-GLT) and
a class-balanced but attribute-wise long-tailed test set (TestCBL), focusing on assessing the model’s ability to handle
class-wise imbalance; 2) Attribute-wise Long Tail (ALT)
Protocol adopts a class-balanced yet attribute-wise longtailed training set (Train-CBL) and a test set (Test-GBL)
balanced in both class and attribute distributions, aiming
to evaluate robustness against attribute-wise imbalance; 3)
Generalized Long Tail (GLT) Protocol combines TrainGLT (dual long-tailed) and Test-GBL (dual balanced), requiring models to address both biases simultaneously for
satisfactory performance, thus serving as the comprehensive
evaluation for the generalized long-tailed challenge.

6. Conclusion
This work investigates the problem of inter-class feature
confusion and intra-class feature dispersion in Generalized
Long-Tailed (GLT) classification, which arises from class
imbalance and significant intra-class attribute variations.
We propose a dual-expert collaborative learning framework
based on the Equiangular Tight Frame (ETF) classifier. The
Long-Tailed Learning Expert (LTLE) captures class-specific
core features and identifies hard-to-learn samples, while
the Hard Sample Learning Expert (HSLE) constrains the
features of hard samples within a classifier-centered neighborhood via the Boundary ETF classifier. Extensive experiments conducted on the ImageNet-GLT and MSCOCO-GLT
datasets demonstrate that the proposed method achieves
state-of-the-art performance across various settings. Moreover, our approach effectively alleviates both class-wise
and attribute-wise biases, validating the effectiveness of the
proposed collaborative strategy for GLT learning.

As illustrated in Figure 7, we visualize the classification
boundaries corresponding to four ETF classifiers in a threedimensional feature space. Samples that fall within these
boundaries can be correctly classified. Since the cosine angles between any pair of ETF classifiers are always greater
than 90◦ , the theoretical maximum discriminative margin is
45◦ . Based on this property, we explore multiple boundary
angles to constrain the distribution of sample features. As
long as the features are restricted within this discriminative
boundary, correct classification can be guaranteed. Experimental results demonstrate that constraining the cosine
similarity of Boundary ETF within cos 45◦ is a reasonable
and effective choice.

D. In-depth Explanation of Class-Specific Components zc and Attribute-Related Variables za
This section provides a systematic and comprehensive explanation of two key components in the Generalized Long-Tail (GLT)
classification scenario: Class-Specific Components (zc ) and Attribute-Related Variables (za ). Their essential definitions,
roles, and impacts on model learning are discussed in detail, highlighting how their interactions lead to classification
challenges. Mathematical formulations are also derived to quantify the influence of zc and za on the classification process.
D.1. Core Definitions and Essential Characteristics
Class-Specific Components zc : Class-Specific Components (zc ) refer to the core, class-invariant features of an image
that are closely tied to the category label Y . These features serve as the primary basis for classifying samples and remain
unchanged across different environments or external attributes. For instance, the zc of the ”dog” category might include the
leg structure, fur texture, and head shape, which remain consistent whether the dog is in a typical setting (such as a grassy
field) or an atypical one (such as a swimming pool). The critical property of zc is its consistent distribution across both
training and test datasets, which ensures robust model performance. This property is formally expressed as:
ptrain (zc | Y ) = ptest (zc | Y )

(34)

The ideal distribution of zc in GLT data follows the Equiangular Tight Frame (ETF) structure, which maximizes inter-class
separability, providing the model with a clear decision boundary between classes.
Attribute-Related Variables za : Attribute-Related Variables (za ) represent the attributes that are unrelated to the class label
but contribute to intra-class variations. These attributes can be object-level (such as color, texture, or pose) or image-level
(such as background, lighting, or shooting angle). Unlike zc , za cannot directly determine the category of an image. For
instance, the difference between a ”white dog” and a ”gray dog” stems from variations in za . Two key characteristics of za
significantly impact GLT classification: first, its distribution tends to be inconsistent between training and test sets, formally
expressed as:
ptrain (za | Y ) ̸= ptest (za | Y )

(35)

Second, za is inherently complex with a nearly limitless combination of possible attribute values. Due to this, balancing the
distribution of za through manual annotations or data augmentation is nearly impossible. This imbalance often induces the
model to learn false correlations between za and category labels.
D.2. Dual Impact Mechanism on Model Learning
The interaction between zc and za presents two main challenges in GLT classification: intra-class feature dispersion and
inter-class feature confusion. These challenges stem from the fact that za interferes with the learning of zc , ultimately
degrading the model’s performance.
Intra-Class Feature Dispersion: The core goal of zc is to ensure that all samples from the same category converge to
a unified class center in the feature space. However, due to the imbalance of za in GLT data, the model learns a mixed
feature representation of zc + za rather than a pure zc . For example, in the ”dog” class, the majority of training samples may
involve dogs in a grassy environment, causing the model to associate the ”grassy background” with the dog’s core features.
When the model encounters a ”dog in a swimming pool,” a rare za combination, the features will deviate from the class
center, leading to intra-class feature dispersion.
Inter-Class Feature Confusion: Spurious correlations induced by za not only affect intra-class learning but also distort the
decision boundaries between classes. This is particularly problematic for tail categories, where sample sizes are small. For
instance, if a ”dog in water” shares similar za features (e.g., aquatic background and streamlined body shape) with the ”seal”
class, the model might misclassify the seal as a dog. This misclassification occurs because the model has not sufficiently
learned the class-specific features of the seal, and the strong influence of za causes confusion across class boundaries.

D.4. Summary: Synergistic Optimization of zc and za
The essence of model training is to decouple the class-specific component zc and the attribute-related variable za from the
mixed feature zx = hθ (x). The optimization objective can be formally expressed as:
(θ∗ , ϕ∗ ) = arg min L(zx − zc , za )
θ,ϕ

(40)

This objective comprises two core aspects:
• Objective 1: Minimize the difference between zx and zc , enabling the model to learn stable class-specific core features.
• Objective 2: Constrain the interference range of za to prevent it from undermining the discriminability of zc .
In GLT classification tasks, zc is the key factor determining model performance, while za is the primary interference factor
leading to performance degradation. To achieve effective feature decoupling, it is necessary to adhere to the collaborative
optimization principle of “strengthening the discriminative power of zc while constraining the interference of za ”. The
specific implementation strategies are as follows:
• Enhancing the discriminability of zc : Adopt the Equiangular Tight Frame (ETF) structure to define the ideal
distribution of zc , and use the Variance-Sensitive (VS) loss to balance inter-class gradients, ensuring the separability of
different class features in the space.
• Constraining the interference range of za : While preserving the diversity of za , utilize the boundary ETF classifier
to restrict the feature variance induced by za within the neighborhood of zc , thereby effectively preventing intra-class
feature dispersion and inter-class feature confusion.
