We study the relation between the total variation
(TV) and Hellinger distances between two Gaussian location mixtures. Our first result establishes
a general upper bound: for any two mixing distributions supported on a compact set, the Hellinger
distance between the two mixtures is controlled
by the TV distance raised to a power 1 − o(1),
where the o(1) term is of order 1/ log log(1/TV).
We also construct two sequences of mixing distributions that demonstrate the sharpness of this
bound. Taken together, our results resolve an open
problem raised in Jia et al. (2023) and thus lead
to an entropic characterization of learning Gaussian mixtures in total variation. Our inequality
also yields optimal robust estimation of Gaussian
mixtures in Hellinger distance, which has a direct
implication for bounding the minimax regret of
empirical Bayes under Huber contamination.

The Hellinger distance is a commonly used loss function for
density estimation. It is especially useful in the setting of
Gaussian location mixture estimation given its direct consequence for bounding the regret of an empirical Bayes estimator using a plug-in estimator of the prior. When the data
set contains a small subset of arbitrary outliers, the density
estimation problem can be regarded as misspecified under
total variation. Therefore, sharp inequalities are necessary
for deriving optimal error rates for robust density estimation
of Gaussian location mixtures, and the inequalities in (1)
are too loose for this purpose.

We establish a sharp relationship between total variation
and Hellinger distances. While most existing work focuses
on minimax upper bounds under Hellinger loss, relatively
little is known for total variation. Our results thus advance
the understanding of total variation for Gaussian mixture
models. In particular, we characterize the minimax estimation rate under total variation via metric entropy and derive
minimax-optimal bounds for robust density estimation under Hellinger loss.

In a typical empirical Bayes setting, one has i.i.d. observations generated by fπ . Here, we consider a more general
data generating process in (22) that allows the presence of
arbitrary outliers. This requires the estimator fb to be robust,
and thus the Yatracos’ estimator satisfying the risk bound in
Theorem 4.5 is adopted here.
We note that the clean data setting of the problem with ϵ = 0
has been well studied in the literature (James et al., 1961;
Efron & Morris, 1972; 1973; Johnstone, 2002; Ignatiadis
& Sen, 2025), and the nonparametric maximum likelihood
estimator (NPMLE) and sieve MLE are shown to achieve
the parametric rate up to some logarithmic factor (Wong &
Shen, 1995; Genovese & Wasserman, 2000; Ghosal & Van
Der Vaart, 2001; Jiang & Zhang, 2009; Saha & Guntuboyina,
2020; Soloff et al., 2025). However, when ϵ > 0, it is
unclear whether the NPMLE still works with presence of
arbitrary outliers. We suspect that the error rate of the
NPMLE has a highly sub-optimal dependence on ϵ.

Our results are derived for d-dimensional isotropic Gaussian
mixture models with covariance fixed to Id . While we discuss implications for empirical Bayes methods, these procedures often involve a joint prior on the location and variance
when the variance is unknown. Extending our analysis to
heteroscedastic Gaussian mixture models and studying the
relationship between total variation and Hellinger distances
in this setting remains an interesting direction for future
work.
Just as the sharp relationship between total variation and
Hellinger distances had remained an open question prior
to this work, the sharp logarithmic relationship between
total variation and L2 distances is still open. Resolving
this question is expected to have significant implications for
nonparametric density estimation. Moreover, establishing
a sharp connection between total variation distance and
Fisher divergence could further deepen the understanding
of empirical Bayes procedures in robust settings.

Soloff et al. (2025) and related papers crucially rely on the
Hellinger control of the Fisher divergence. See Theorem

The first is the Nikolskii-type inequality. In case d = 1, the Nikolskii-type inequality has been extensively studied. For

well; for example, see Lemma 5 of Maizlish & Prymak (2015).
The third, to the best of our knowledge, does not have a standard name, but it can be derived as a combination of the
